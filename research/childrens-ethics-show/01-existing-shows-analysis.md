# Existing Children's Shows Analysis: Teaching Ethics to 9-12 Year Olds

This document analyzes successful (and unsuccessful) approaches to teaching ethics and morality in children's media, with a focus on the tween demographic (ages 9-12).

---

## Part 1: Successful Examples

### Avatar: The Last Airbender (2005-2008)
**Target Age:** 8-14 | **Format:** 61 episodes, 22 minutes each

**Why It Works:**

1. **Moral Complexity Through Character Arcs**
   - Zuko's redemption arc is widely considered one of the best in television history
   - His journey is non-linear: "Halfway through the series, he may be at a 7 [on a good scale]. But then a couple of episodes later, he is pushed back to a 5"
   - This reflects real moral growth—people don't progress in straight lines

2. **Eastern Philosophy Integration**
   - Deeply influenced by Confucianism: balance, harmony, collective good
   - Interconnectedness prioritized over individualistic pursuits
   - The four nations represent different philosophical approaches to living

3. **The Nonviolent Hero**
   - Aang doesn't like fighting—finding nonviolent answers is his core struggle
   - "To find nonviolent answers to violent problems is a daunting task—it requires dodging attacks, thinking quickly and growing in maturity"
   - His belief that all life is important creates genuine moral tension (especially in the finale)

4. **Villains with Understandable Motivations**
   - Zuko begins as antagonist but is driven by "desperate need for acceptance and love from his family"
   - Even in early episodes, glimpses of inner conflict and compassion shine through
   - "This duality creates a character whose struggle between right and wrong feels authentic and deeply human"

5. **The "Moral Vertigo" Technique**
   - Characters achieve what they thought they wanted, then realize it doesn't satisfy
   - Zuko returns home with everything—title, respect, father's love—but "isn't happy"
   - This teaches that goals must align with values

**Key Episodes for Analysis:**
- "Zuko Alone" — Zuko sees firsthand the destruction his nation has caused
- "The Day of Black Sun, Part 2" — Zuko abandons selfish motivations for selfless endeavors
- Series finale — Aang's moral refusal to kill even a genocidal villain

**Sources:** [Philosophy Through Zuko (BYU)](https://hum.byu.edu/philosophy-through-zuko), [Analyzing Zuko's Redemption Arc (ResearchGate)](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/397946009_Analyzing_Zuko's_Redemption_Arc_in_Avatar_The_Last_Airbender_Through_Maslow's_Hierarchy_of_Needs)

### Steven Universe (2013-2019)
**Target Age:** 8-14 | **Format:** 160 episodes + movie + sequel series

**Why It Works:**

1. **Shades of Gray Instead of Mustache-Twirling Villains**
   - "Deals with issues of extraordinary violence and horror, depicts its characters in shades of grey"
   - "Subtly plays with matters of philosophy, morality, and interpersonal conflicts"
   - Refuses to reset development to a status quo

2. **Healthy vs. Unhealthy Relationships**
   - Core focus on "the difference between healthy and unhealthy relationships"
   - Characters model conflict resolution, communication, consent
   - Multiple relationship types explored (romantic, familial, platonic)

3. **LGBTQ+ Representation Without Homophobia**
   - "Hidden representation for LGBTQ characters of different shapes, sizes, colors and planets"
   - Crucially: the show's world doesn't include homophobia—queerness is simply normal
   - Creator Rebecca Sugar: "I often hear from viewers who say it helped them understand themselves"

4. **Emotional Intelligence as Superpower**
   - Steven's power is empathy and connection, not strength
   - Villains are often reformed through understanding rather than defeat
   - Mental health treated with nuance (Steven goes to therapy in sequel series)

5. **"Fitting In" as Moral Journey**
   - Teaches "strong-will power, feminism, independence, being yourself"
   - Characters struggle with identity, purpose, belonging
   - Resolution comes through self-acceptance rather than external validation

**Notable Technique:** Musical episodes that crystallize emotional truths

**Sources:** [Steven Universe Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steven_Universe), [Common Sense Media Review](https://www.commonsensemedia.org/tv-reviews/steven-universe)

### Bluey (2018-present)
**Target Age:** 4-8 (but resonates with all ages) | **Format:** 7 minutes each

**Why It Works:**

1. **Emotional Intelligence Over Academic Learning**
   - In "Chest" episode: "helping children gain knowledge is great, but during younger years, it might be best to focus on their hearts"
   - Prioritizes compassion, kindness, imagination over facts

2. **Adults Model Failure and Growth**
   - "Nobody is above it—not even Bandit or Chilli—because it's normal!"
   - Adults make mistakes, apologize, learn
   - "Muffin Cone" episode: parent criticizes child's lack of self-control while eating an entire bag of chips

3. **Philosophy Through Play**
   - Lessons about impermanence, loss, friendship all through imaginative games
   - Jean Luc episode teaches "even if friends grow apart, each person can learn and grow from the times shared together"

4. **Respects Parents (Counter-Cultural)**
   - "An important diversion from the typical way parents are depicted in kids' TV shows, which is usually some combination of stupid, naive, and clueless"
   - Validates multiple parenting styles without judgment

5. **Letting Children Problem-Solve**
   - "Bin Night": parents give advice but let daughter work through conflict independently
   - "The important thing is they persevere and aren't too proud to apologize when needed"

**Key Insight:** Short format forces crystallized lessons without preachiness

**Sources:** [The Ethics Institute on Bluey](https://cssh.northeastern.edu/ethics/what-parents-not-just-children-can-learn-from-watching-bluey/), [ScreenRant Life Lessons](https://screenrant.com/bluey-best-life-lessons/)

### The Good Place (2016-2020)
**Target Age:** 13+ (but techniques apply) | **Format:** 53 episodes, 22 minutes each

**Why It Works (Adaptable Techniques):**

1. **Philosophy Made Accessible Through Comedy**
   - "An engaging, funny and surprisingly accessible introduction to moral philosophy"
   - Uses thought experiments (trolley problem) but makes them tangible in the plot
   - Michael: "I made the trolley problem real so that we could see how the ethics would actually play out"

2. **Student-Teacher Dynamic**
   - Chidi (philosophy professor) teaches Eleanor (self-interested person)
   - "Can we really learn how to be good through academic teaching?" drives the show
   - Answer: virtue is learned through practice, like a musical instrument (citing Aristotle)

3. **Narrative Ethics as Structure**
   - "Impressive weaving of philosophical content with comedy—a vivid embodiment of narrative ethics at work"
   - Philosophy is "part of what it is to be a flourishing person," not just academic

4. **Incremental Moral Growth**
   - Eleanor doesn't become good overnight—she becomes "more considerate and principled"
   - The series provides a narrative arc around ethical development

**Adaptable for Younger Audiences:**
- The Socratic dialogue format (questions, not answers)
- Making abstract philosophy concrete through story
- Characters who model learning ethics in real-time

**Sources:** [APA Blog on The Good Place](https://blog.apaonline.org/2020/01/28/a-good-place-for-philosophy/), [KQED Analysis](https://www.kqed.org/pop/105436/what-the-good-place-has-taught-us-about-philosophy-and-life-so-far)

### Arthur (1996-2022)
**Target Age:** 4-8 | **Format:** 25 seasons, 11 minutes each

**Why It Works:**

1. **Research-Backed Perspective-Taking**
   - UC Davis research: "Children who watched an episode of Arthur with the moral lesson scored higher on measures of perspective-taking"

2. **Telling Kids the Truth**
   - Creator Marc Brown: "One of the best things we've done with Arthur is tell kids the truth. That doesn't happen enough in the media"

3. **Diverse Cast Modeling Social Skills**
   - "Features a diverse group of characters who learn about respect, tolerance, friendship, hard work, patience"
   - Family dynamics explored through sibling relationships

4. **Surprisingly Mature Themes**
   - Parents note it tackles "deep themes such as politics, diversity, and emotional challenges"
   - Suitable for older children despite young target demo

**Caveat:** Research also found that conflict depiction (characters being unkind) sometimes backfires with youngest viewers—see Pitfalls section.

**Sources:** [PBS Parents on Arthur](https://www.pbs.org/parents/thrive/the-moral-of-the-story-arthur-helps-kids-build-character), [Arthur Wiki Morals List](https://arthur.fandom.com/wiki/List_of_morals)

### Gravity Falls (2012-2016)
**Target Age:** 9-14 | **Format:** 40 episodes, 22 minutes each

**Why It Works:**

1. **Internal Conflict as Primary Drama**
   - "Most conflicts are internal. Usually, the show establishes an external conflict, but by the end, the character becomes adapted to their world and deals with their flaws"

2. **Growing Up as Adventure**
   - "Proposes growing up as its own adventure. The idea that in the 'real world' there is no wonder or mystery is entirely false"

3. **Trust and Vulnerability**
   - Journal 3 warns Dipper to "trust no one"—he learns this is wrong
   - "When you battle a hundred gnomes side-by-side with someone, you realize that they've probably always got your back"

4. **Self-Worth Independent of Relationships**
   - "Being single does not equal being alone"
   - Characters learn "what matters is what you think of yourself, and what the people you love think of you"

5. **Precise Visual Storytelling**
   - "Uses its 'camera' as a storytelling tool more precisely than most, if not all other comedy and animated shows"

**Sources:** [Teen Magazine on Gravity Falls](https://www.theteenmagazine.com/10-lessons-from-10-years-of-gravity-falls), [Wanderer's Pen Writing Lessons](https://victoriagracehowell.com/writing-lessons-from-tv-shows-gravity/)

### She-Ra and the Princesses of Power (2018-2020)
**Target Age:** 8-14 | **Format:** 52 episodes, 22 minutes each

**Why It Works:**

1. **Moral Ambiguity in Characters**
   - Double Trouble: "inhabits a complicated moral tapestry... not just there to be the moral of the story, but to advance the story, to escalate conflict"
   - "Full personhood looks like being able to play characters that aren't just there to be the moral"

2. **Never Talking Down to Kids**
   - Creator Stevenson: "I don't believe in talking down to kids... I don't believe in trying to sanitize things, because kids really do understand complicated themes"

3. **Queering the Hero's Journey**
   - "Queering normative understandings of heroic gender and sexuality"
   - Protagonist Adora "grapples with the simple binaries of good and evil and her own binary identity"

4. **No In-World Oppression of Queerness**
   - "Refusal to include themes of homophobia"—queerness is normal, not a source of conflict
   - Allows LGBTQ+ representation without trauma narratives

**Sources:** [NPR on She-Ra](https://www.npr.org/2020/05/15/854610573/in-she-ra-and-the-princesses-of-power-true-strength-is-in-being-yourself), [Toon Boom on Queer Representation](https://www.toonboom.com/how-she-ra-broke-ground-for-queer-representation-in-childrens-media)

### Hilda (2018-2023)
**Target Age:** 7-12 | **Format:** 3 seasons + movie

**Why It Works:**

1. **Doesn't Pander or Talk Down**
   - "Hilda doesn't pander with moral lessons or talk down to its viewers—child or adult"
   - "The pacing never approaches the manic action of the average kid's show. The story has room to breathe"

2. **Love as Preparation for Fear**
   - "Hilda's courage and readiness to sacrifice herself for others become more certain after each encounter"
   - "Her love is, in essence, preparing her to face each new fear"

3. **Folklore-Rich World Building**
   - Inspired by Nordic folklore—trolls, elves, giants
   - Supernatural and mundane coexist naturally

4. **Compassion as Central Theme**
   - "Teaches children and viewers about compassion and communication"
   - When characters offer love to those who don't warrant it, "the results are often wondrous"

**Sources:** [Netflix Hilda Review (Collider)](https://collider.com/hilda-netflix-review/), [Think Christian on Hilda](https://thinkchristian.net/in-hilda-perfect-love-drives-out-fear-and-trolls)

### Over the Garden Wall (2014)
**Target Age:** 10-14 | **Format:** 10 episodes, 11 minutes each (miniseries)

**Why It Works:**

1. **Fairy Tale Moral Ambiguity**
   - "In privileging imagery and mood over lessons, Over the Garden Wall captures something vanishingly rare in children's media: the moral ambiguity of fairy tale worlds"

2. **Character Flaws Drive Plot**
   - "Forces Wirt and Greg to overcome their personal shortcomings in order to escape danger"
   - Wirt (worrywart, indecisive) and Greg (oblivious) must grow

3. **Each Episode as Parable**
   - "Each episode acts as a kind of fable or parable, using a simple narrative to convey moral lessons through bizarre, impossible situations"

4. **Modern Characters in Timeless Setting**
   - "Characters are distinctly modern—Wirt is a teenager entirely of the 2010s—but much of the show has a timeless appeal"

**Sources:** [3 Brothers Film Analysis](https://3brothersfilm.com/blog/2017/11/15/over-the-garden-wall-is-a-fairy-tale-for-the-adventure-time-generation), [MDPI: Motifs and Moral Ambiguity](https://www.mdpi.com/2076-0787/5/3/51)

## Part 2: What Makes Them Work (Synthesis)

### 1. Non-Linear Moral Growth
The best shows depict ethics as a journey with setbacks, not a destination. Characters make progress, regress, and learn again. This mirrors real moral development.

### 2. Villains with Understandable Motivations
Mustache-twirling evil is boring and teaches nothing. Antagonists driven by recognizable desires (love, acceptance, security) teach children that "bad people" are often just people making bad choices.

### 3. Internal Conflict as Primary Drama
The most memorable episodes pit characters against themselves—their fears, insecurities, biases—rather than external foes.

### 4. Respect for Audience Intelligence
Shows that trust children to understand complexity outperform those that simplify. As She-Ra's creator said: "Kids really do understand complicated themes."

### 5. Emotional Intelligence as Superpower
Whether it's Steven's empathy, Aang's pacifism, or Hilda's compassion—the best shows make emotional/moral skills as powerful as physical abilities.

### 6. Room to Breathe
Slower pacing allows themes to land without being stated explicitly. Shows that rush leave no space for reflection.

### 7. Adults Who Model Growth
When adults admit mistakes and learn, children see that ethics is a lifelong practice, not something you master.

### 8. Diversity Without Tokenism
Successful shows integrate diverse characters and perspectives organically—they're present because the world is diverse, not because there's a "diversity lesson."

## Part 3: Age-Appropriate Approaches for Tweens (9-12)

### Cognitive Development at This Age
According to Kohlberg's stages of moral development:
- **Stage 3 (Interpersonal Accord):** Children start caring what others think, internalize role models' standards
- **Stage 4 (Maintaining Social Order):** Understanding of broader societal rules, questioning authority if part of their culture

### Key Developmental Insights

1. **Complexity is Appropriate**
   - By age 9-10, children can understand motives and intentions, not just outcomes
   - They can follow non-linear moral arcs
   - They can handle moral ambiguity

2. **Peer Reference Matters**
   - "The reference group for children's moral beliefs increasingly focuses on other children"
   - Shows with ensemble casts of peers (Gravity Falls, Avatar) resonate

3. **They Question Rules**
   - Children this age recognize "rules are made by people and can be changed if everyone agrees"
   - Shows that question authority (not just obey it) connect with this development

4. **Perspective-Taking Is Emerging**
   - "As children mature cognitively, they become better at seeing things from another person's perspective"
   - Complex antagonists support this development

### What Tweens Need vs. Younger Children

| Younger (4-8) | Tweens (9-12) |
|---------------|---------------|
| Clear heroes and villains | Morally complex characters |
| Explicit lessons stated | Lessons embedded in narrative |
| Simple cause-effect | Non-linear consequences |
| Authority figures are right | Authority can be questioned |
| "What should I do?" | "Why should I do it?" |
| Rules to follow | Principles to understand |
| Individual actions | Systemic thinking |

**Sources:** [Piaget's Theory of Moral Development](https://www.simplypsychology.org/piaget-moral.html), [Moral Development in Adolescence](https://www.mentalhealth.com/library/adolescent-moral-development)

## Part 4: Pitfalls to Avoid

### Captain Planet (1990-1996): The Cautionary Tale

**What Went Wrong:**

1. **Sledgehammer Approach**
   - "Like being hit over the head with a sledgehammer with the words 'THE LESSON' etched on the side"
   - Every episode was essentially a 22-minute PSA stretched thin

2. **Cartoonishly Evil Villains**
   - "Paints environmental damage as a result of deliberate pollution, which is far from the truth"
   - Real pollution comes from neglect and profit motives—not cackling villains
   - This makes the lesson less applicable to real life

3. **Contradictory Message About Agency**
   - "The show tries to tell us that anyone can make a difference, but in the end, the Planeteers can't accomplish anything without calling forth Captain Planet"
   - Compare: Care Bears "beat the enemy through caring rather than brute force"

4. **Tackling Complex Topics Ham-Fistedly**
   - Episodes on AIDS, gang warfare were "almost comically tone deaf"
   - Serious issues became "ham-fisted morals"

5. **Oversimplifying Systemic Problems**
   - "The power is yours!" has been criticized as climate change is largely caused by governments and corporations
   - Puts burden of systemic problems on individual children

**Sources:** [TV Tropes: Captain Planet YMMV](https://tvtropes.org/pmwiki/pmwiki.php/YMMV/CaptainPlanetAndThePlaneteers), [IMDb User Reviews](https://www.imdb.com/title/tt0098763/reviews/)

### The "Backfire Effect" in Educational Television

UC Davis research revealed critical problems with how many shows structure moral lessons:

1. **The 9-3 Problem**
   - "In 12 minutes of content, children often see nine minutes of exclusionary behavior or a problem being presented with only three minutes or less of a solution"
   - Solutions don't resonate; the problem behavior is what children remember

2. **Nuance Backfiring**
   - "Nuance may backfire, causing children to behave poorly in their own lives because they don't understand the nuanced solutions"
   - Complex solutions require more time or explicit unpacking

3. **Arthur Paradox**
   - "Even though educational shows like Arthur have pro-education and pro-social goals, conflict between characters is often depicted with characters being unkind"
   - "Children who spent more time watching educational programs increased their relational aggression"

4. **Children Miss the Moral**
   - "Preschool children really don't get the moral of the story because that requires that they understand how all the parts of the show fit together"
   - "The ability to articulate a moral theme develops fairly slowly, emerging only around age 9 or 10"

**Solutions from Research:**
- "Just putting 30 seconds of explanation in the program helped the children to understand"
- Literal messaging (not metaphorical) promoted prosocial intentions
- Co-viewing with adults who discuss content dramatically improves outcomes

**Sources:** [UC Davis Research](https://www.ucdavis.edu/news/moral-lessons-childrens-television-programs-may-require-extra-explanation-be-effective), [Futurity: Subtle Messages Backfire](https://www.futurity.org/childrens-tv-shows-morals-inclusiveness-2096832-2/)

### Common Pitfalls to Avoid

| Pitfall | Example | Alternative |
|---------|---------|-------------|
| **Preachiness** | Captain Planet, much 80s animation | Let story carry message; trust audience |
| **Perfect protagonists** | Mary Sue characters | Heroes with flaws they must overcome |
| **One-dimensional villains** | Generic "evil for evil's sake" | Antagonists with relatable motivations |
| **Problem > Solution time** | Many educational shows | Balance problem/solution screen time |
| **Reset to status quo** | Many sitcoms | Permanent consequences and growth |
| **Adults as idiots** | Most Disney Channel live action | Competent adults who still make mistakes |
| **"Very Special Episodes"** | Many 90s shows | Integrate themes throughout series |
| **Individual solutions to systemic problems** | Captain Planet | Show complexity of real-world issues |
| **Talking down to kids** | Heavy exposition | Trust children to infer meaning |
| **Metaphors without grounding** | Abstract lessons | Make themes concrete in story |

## Part 5: Key Techniques Summary

### Narrative Techniques That Work

1. **Show the struggle, not just the lesson**
   - Characters wrestle with decisions on-screen
   - Moral reasoning is part of the drama

2. **Non-linear redemption arcs**
   - Progress, setback, progress—like real life
   - Zuko-style complexity

3. **Ensemble perspectives**
   - Multiple characters with different moral frameworks
   - Debate and dialogue between worldviews

4. **Consequences that persist**
   - No reset buttons
   - Choices matter

5. **Villains who could be heroes**
   - Antagonists one bad day away from protagonists
   - Redemption always possible

6. **Adults who model learning**
   - Not perfect authorities
   - Show growth is lifelong

### Character Archetypes That Teach

1. **The Reluctant Hero** (Aang, Wirt)
   - Didn't ask for responsibility but must accept it
   - Teaches: Duty, courage, sacrifice

2. **The Redeemable Antagonist** (Zuko, Catra)
   - Started on wrong side, finds their way
   - Teaches: Change is possible, judge actions not people

3. **The Empathic Center** (Steven, Hilda)
   - Power through connection, not force
   - Teaches: Emotional intelligence as strength

4. **The Flawed Mentor** (Iroh, Chidi)
   - Wise but not perfect
   - Teaches: Learning never stops

5. **The Peer Group** (Team Avatar, Crystal Gems)
   - Diverse strengths and weaknesses
   - Teaches: Collaboration, complementary skills

## Part 6: Implications for New Shows

Based on this analysis, a children's ethics show for 9-12 year olds should:

### Do
- Trust the audience to handle complexity
- Create morally nuanced antagonists
- Show non-linear moral growth with setbacks
- Let story carry the message (not dialogue)
- Give adequate time to solutions, not just problems
- Include diverse perspectives and debates
- Make protagonists flawed in specific, relatable ways
- Allow permanent consequences
- Integrate themes throughout the series (avoid "very special episodes")
- Consider ensemble casts with peer characters

### Avoid
- Stating lessons explicitly through dialogue
- Creating villains without understandable motivations
- Presenting individual solutions to systemic problems
- Heavy-handed "issue" episodes
- Perfect protagonists
- Status quo resets
- Talking down to the audience
- More problem time than solution time
- Adults as incompetent or irrelevant

### Consider
- Musical/artistic elements for emotional crystallization (Steven Universe)
- Student-teacher dynamics for explicit philosophy (The Good Place)
- Limited series format for tight thematic focus (Over the Garden Wall)
- Folklore/myth structure for timeless resonance
- Co-viewing guides or discussion materials
